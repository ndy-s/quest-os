# Key Decisions — Japanese Fluency

> These are settled. Don't revisit them weekly.

---

**Should I use Duolingo?**
Decision: as a warmup only, not as a primary tool.
Rationale: Duolingo is not sufficient for N3. It's fine for 5 minutes of morning activation, but it won't get you to the vocabulary or grammar coverage you need. Anki + Bunpro + Genki is the primary stack.

---

**Should I learn romaji?**
Decision: no. Stop using romaji after week 2.
Rationale: Romaji creates a crutch that actively slows down kana reading fluency. Two weeks is enough to learn hiragana and katakana. After that, force yourself to read in kana. It's uncomfortable for about a week, then it sticks.

---

**Anki vs Wanikani for kanji?**
Decision: Anki for vocab (Core 2000 deck), add Wanikani from Month 3 if budget allows.
Rationale: Anki with a frequency-based deck is more efficient for general vocab. Wanikani's mnemonics are genuinely better for kanji retention, but it's a paid subscription. Start with Anki; add Wanikani in Month 3 once you know the system is working for you.

---

**Should I try to speak from Day 1?**
Decision: no. Speaking starts in Month 6.
Rationale: Forced output before you have enough input creates bad habits and anxiety. Months 1–5 build the foundation. By Month 6 you'll have enough vocabulary and grammar to have a basic conversation — that's the right time to start speaking practice.

---

**JLPT N5 first, or go straight for N3?**
Decision: attempt N5 in December 2026 as a checkpoint, then N3 in July 2027.
Rationale: N5 in December is a low-stakes way to validate your progress and get familiar with the exam format. If you skip N5, you might arrive at N3 in June 2027 not knowing whether your level is actually exam-ready. The checkpoint is worth the registration fee.
